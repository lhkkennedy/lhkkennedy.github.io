Public Debt

The UK owes a lot of money

Understanding our public debt Data Sciece project

Governments across the globe have enacted massive spending packages financed by a lot of public debt, in response to the COVID-19 pandemic. A question often asked is: how is the government able to borrow so much cash and can the tax payers pay it all back.


The UK owes £2.2 trillion or roughly £32,000 per person in public sector debts. It’s the equivalent to 103.6% of GDP for the 2021 financial year. That’s a result of high rates of public sector borrowing; they had borrowed £323.9 billion or 15.1% of GDP in the same financial year (ONS, Oct 2021).


How can the government afford to accrue massive amounts of debt and is there a limit? This project explores through a number of visualisations the state of the UK's public sector debt.

How much are we borrowing?

The UK government has the capacity, when it requires, to spend more than it can raise in tax revenue. This is normally done during a crisis and spending is financed through public borrowing. 2020 has seen record levels of borrowing - substantially more than the borrowing in 2009 during the financial crash. In the 2019/20 financial year the UK had borrowed £272.879 Billion while borrowing in 2009 was £160 Billion.




Although the current budget deficit and borrowing are correlated, we can also see that borrowing had not yet completely tapered off following the 2008 financial crash. The fall in the deficit is the result of austerity measures shrinking the public sector post-financial crash. This was in part due to fears about the ballooning debt levels.

Is that a lot?

The level of public sector debt has reached levels last seen in early 1960s. (FT, 2021)

Austerity measures were introduced to help service the debt post-crash. However even before the pandemic, levels of debt were still rising. This is because borrowing continued for the 12 years leading up to the pandemic. Some economist say that the austerity measures amongst factors such as the eurozone crisis, culminated in less spending and growth; culminating in less tax revenues to service the debt. This was eventually reversed with substantial tax increases.(IFS, 2019)
Leading governments have continued to borrow and spend at record levels as they and central banks have begun adopted new thinking about how much they are able to borrow sustainably. Central banks have supported the government’s fiscal efforts by increasing the money supply. And an increase in saving rates have kept interest rates low. With record low interest rates the past two decades it has been very easy for GDP to outpace the interest payments of debt. See below for more on interest rates.

Conclusion:

Understanding our public debt Data Sciece project

Debt levels are most definetly at their highest levels in recent history, though it is not uprecedented, as the WWII & I wartime economies across the globe took up much higher debt-to-GDP levels. High public sector debt levels and increased money printing has generally been something to avoid. However, governments now seem to be more comfortable with higher debt levels. Asserting that the borrowing practices are sustainable as long as interest rates remain low.


The financial times has reported that interest payments were much higher than forecasted in 2021 as inflation rapidly rises. So, there is concern about how much longer central banks will be able to support fiscal policy with QE and turn their attention to monetary issues. If RPI continues to rise at record rates the government will be forced to cutdown spending to ensure servicing of the debt payments.

How does the UK compare to the rest of the world in debt-to-GDP ratio?
The countries with highest debt-to-GDP ratios are countries such as Venezuela which holds 350% of GDP in public debt - Japan at 266% of GDP - Sudan at 259% and Greece at 206%. That means the government in these countries will have to eventually service debts equivalent to every finished good and service produced in the country over two years or three in the case of Venezuela.
The UK can be seen 30th on the list. The US is 11th while Russia and the Cayman Islands are 165th and 172nd respectively. Although the UK has been increased its borrowing massively due to the larger size of GDP, the Debt-to-GDP ratio does not reach levels like Greece or Venezuela.

Scraper

A python script was created to scrape the country name and their respective debt to gdp ratios that was displayed in a table at https://tradingeconomics.com/country-list/government-debt-to-gdp. The data was placed in a dataframe then formatted and exported as a csv file. 
   import pandas 
   as pd
   import requests
   import os
   from bs4 import BeautifulSoup
   
   #downloading the source code for the URL then searching for a table
   URL = "https://tradingeconomics.com/country-list/government-debt-to-gdp"
   html = requests.get(URL)
   soup = BeautifulSoup(html.content, 'html.parser')
   table = soup.find_all("table", class_="table table-hover table-heatmap")
   #searching through the tables source code to find the rows.
   table_rows = table[0].find_all('tr')
   
   df = pd.DataFrame()
   # I iterate through the array of rows and extract the country names and values
   # Countries with multiple words required special treatment 
   # They would take up multiple places in the row array shifting the value place
   for tr in table_rows:
       s = table_rows.index(tr)
       items = tr.text
       # Split the text elements in each row text into an array
       arr = items.split()
       extra = len(arr) - 5
       country = arr[0]
       value = arr[1+extra]
       # If the coutntry name is longer than one word we loop until all words are joined.
       for x in range(extra):
           country = country + ' ' + arr[x+1]
       results = [country, value]
       print(results)
       # The data is placed into a data frame to be formated
       df_t = pd.DataFrame(results)
       df_t.columns = ['Data']
       df[s] = df_t['Data']
   
   # Transpose and remove the first row.
   df = df.T
   df = df.iloc[1: , :]
   df.columns = ['Country', 'Debt-to-GDP']
   print(df)
   
   #save to csv
   filename = 'Debt-to-GDP.csv'
   abspath = os.path.abspath(__file__)
   dname = os.path.dirname(abspath)
   os.chdir(dname)
   filelocation = os.getcwd()
   print("Saving as:  [ " + filename + " ]  in:  [ " + filelocation + " ] ")
   df.to_csv(filename)
